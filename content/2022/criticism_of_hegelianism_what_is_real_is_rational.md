# Criticism of Hegelianism: "What is Real is Rational"

> 2022-11-19

A critique of Hegel's famous proposition — _"All that is real is rational; and all that is rational is real"_ — through the lens of Engels' refutation and dialectical materialism.

## The Problem with "What Exists is Reasonable"

Few philosophical propositions have achieved the casual, near-universal adoption of Hegel's dictum. Much like how "human nature" has become the catch-all explanation for social contradiction in modern discourse, "what exists is reasonable" has become the default terminal answer for social phenomena and personal experience alike. People seem compelled to grant their circumstances a rational justification — and this impulse gives Hegel's proposition its persistent foothold.

## Engels' Refutation

From _Ludwig Feuerbach and the End of Classical German Philosophy_ (Friedrich Engels):

> "According to Hegel, everything that is real is rational. But according to the Hegelian dialectic itself, this proposition turns into its opposite: everything that is real in the sphere of human history becomes irrational with the passage of time, is therefore irrational by its very nature, and contains irrationality from the outset; and everything that is rational in the minds of men is destined to become real, however much it may contradict existing apparent reality."

By the rules of Hegel's own dialectical method, the proposition "what is real is rational" transforms into: **"Everything that exists deserves to perish."**

## Analysis

Reality, in its unfolding, manifests as **necessity** — not as proof of rationality. Engels demolished this argument over a century ago, yet it remains relevant for discussion today. In the 21st century's ongoing modernization, many still subscribe to Hegelianism — some genuinely convinced, others having absorbed it passively from their intellectual environment. A moment of critical reflection is enough to produce doubt; that doubt is itself the product of dialectical thinking, an intellectual rejection of Hegel's premise.

### Historical Example: Feudalism

The feudal hierarchy was replaced by capitalism. In its historical development, feudalism necessarily generated the forces that overthrew it — forces that negated it entirely, consigning it to the past. This demonstrates that feudalism was **not reasonable** for human society. If it were, it would have persisted. But reality shows otherwise.

### The Logical Conclusion

If "what exists is reasonable" holds, then anything real in human history — having proven its reality through necessity — must be permanently reasonable, never superseded, existing forever. This implies the **cessation of development**. But things change and develop through contradiction. Hegelianism thus collapses into tautology:

> "What exists, exists." — Because necessity in its unfolding still only proves reality, not rationality.

And ultimately, Hegel's proposition becomes its own negation:

> **"Everything that exists deserves to perish."**

Let us return to Engels' conclusion: _Everything that now exists, however real it may appear, is destined to be superseded — and is therefore, in the dialectical sense, already condemned._
