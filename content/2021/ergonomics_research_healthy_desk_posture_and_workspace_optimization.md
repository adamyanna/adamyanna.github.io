# Ergonomics Research: Healthy Desk Posture & Workspace Optimization

> 2021-02-15

A research-driven guide to maintaining healthy posture during long desk sessions, workspace ergonomics, and personal experience with shoulder nerve pain recovery.

## Correct Sitting Posture

| Checkpoint | Target |
|---|---|
| Feet | Flat on floor, full contact |
| Knee angle | ≥ 90° between calves and thighs |
| Seat depth | 3–5 cm of thigh beyond the seat edge |
| Lumbar support | Lower back fully supported |
| Head/neck | Headrest supporting the cervical spine |
| Hip angle | 90°–110° between torso and thighs (larger = less spinal pressure) |
| Monitor height | Eyes level with upper third of display |
| Armrest height | Forearms at 90°–110°, shoulders relaxed |
| Keyboard tilt | Slight angle for natural wrist position |

**Prolonged sitting is inherently harmful.** Reset your posture whenever you notice yourself slouching — that's your body signaling it needs a change. Alternate every 40 minutes: 5-minute walk or 20-minute standing session.

## Common Issues & Solutions

### Problem: Unmodified Ergonomic Chair

- Large seat pan compresses thighs
- Insufficient lumbar support
- Armrest height poorly adjusted → shoulder strain
- Flat mouse surface forces arm into an uncomfortable angle → right arm/shoulder compression

### Trial & Error Log

| Setup | Issue |
|---|---|
| Standing desk converter | Raised overall desk height → forced chair + armrests higher → shoulder pain after a full day; actual standing time was minimal |
| Hard seat backrest | Seat depth too short (5–10 cm thigh overhang); hard backrest uncomfortable for reclined sitting → neck/shoulder tension; hard surface compressed back muscles when leaning |

### Recommended Setup

1. **Portable laptop riser** — Adjustable for both sitting (screen at eye level) and standing (15–20 min sessions with light stretching). A laptop is sufficient for typical standing work periods.
2. **12 cm latex back cushion** — Keeps thigh overhang at 3–5 cm from seat edge; provides comfortable lumbar support in both upright and reclined positions.
3. **Keyboard with positive tilt** — Slight angle for natural wrist positioning; pair with a firm mousepad and angled mouse platform.
4. **Headrest** — Supports the neck when leaning back, preventing the "cervical shelf" effect from the backrest pushing shoulders forward.
5. **Standing ergonomic mouse** — A vertical/angled mouse reduces forearm pronation and shoulder strain.

### Office Essentials Checklist

- Portable laptop riser (lightweight, easy to relocate)
- Monitor stand/riser
- Custom mechanical keyboard + wrist rest
- Ergonomic (vertical) mouse
- Latex back cushion + neck pillow for chair fit
- Folding bed (for lunch breaks)
- Indoor slippers, water bottle, tea

**Principle:** Portable, low-cost, effective.

## Shoulder Nerve Pain: Case Study

### Episode 2

**Suspected root cause:** Incorrect barbell squat form placing pressure on the cervical spine, leading to referred nerve pain in the right shoulder.

### Intervention

1. **Stop all exercises that load the cervical spine** — Discontinue barbell squats; switch to leg press, hack squat, Bulgarian split squats, and other equipment-based alternatives that don't load the neck.
2. **Medication + physical therapy** — Allow cervical compression to heal; nerve recovery takes time.
3. **Monitor correlation** — Track whether sitting posture, mouse usage, or other desk habits correlate with pain flare-ups. Adjust workspace accordingly.

### Retrospective Analysis

- **Episode 1 (prior):** Triggered by repeated deadlifts + squats, combined with a poorly fitted ergonomic chair and suboptimal mouse posture (unconscious arm elevation).
- **Episode 2 (current):** Again triggered by deadlifts + squats, plus a desk relocation that changed chair/mouse ergonomics. Physical therapist confirmed cervical origin — pressure on the neck referring pain to the right shoulder.

**Key insight:** The root cause is cervical compression, not the shoulder itself. Avoid all neck-loading activities: heavy barbell squats (bar on traps/neck), prolonged phone-downward head angle ("tech neck"), reclined slouching that compresses the cervical spine, and any gym exercise where the bar rests on the neck.

**Alternative leg training** (no spinal loading): Leg press, hack squat, Bulgarian split squat, leg extension, leg curl, belt squat. Squats are replaceable — the long-term health of your neck and shoulders is not.
